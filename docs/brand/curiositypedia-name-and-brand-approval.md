# T04: CuriosityPedia name and brand approval

**Status:** Name approved by owner; repository rename authorized July 18, 2026  
**Research date:** July 18, 2026  
**Scope:** Preliminary name screening and initial brand directions only. This document is evidence for a product decision, not legal clearance.

## Decision

**CuriosityPedia is the final product name.** The canonical display spelling is **CuriosityPedia**; machine-readable identifiers use `curiositypedia`.

The name has excellent immediate fit: it joins curiosity with a recognizable reference-work suffix and accurately suggests a place for exploratory learning. However, the exact coined term appeared in a 2018 Google Research paper for a closely related idea: a question-organized “curiosity web” whose community-driven form was described as “CuriosityPedia.” That is unusually close in both name and concept to this product's branching, question-led learning model. Near-identical **Curiouspedia** has also been used for knowledge and learning projects.

No exact **CuriosityPedia** or **Curiouspedia** wordmark result appeared in the USPTO search performed for this screening, and several useful domains did not return registered records through RDAP. These remain preliminary evidence rather than legal clearance.

### Owner direction

- Apply the CuriosityPedia name throughout the repository.
- Keep the existing production domain and current external URLs working.
- Do not purchase or register a new domain at this stage.
- Retain the research findings below as risk evidence, not as legal clearance.

## Name validation

### Fit

**Strong.** “Curiosity” names the user's motivation; “-pedia” signals organized learning and reference. The combination fits a product that turns one question into a branching, source-backed journey and preserves the path.

The downside is category compression. “-pedia” can make the product sound like a conventional encyclopedia, while the distinctive experience is a living route through researched questions. It may also imply completeness or settled authority that a live, AI-assisted research product should not claim.

### Spelling and pronunciation

Suggested pronunciation: **kyur-ee-OSS-ih-tee-PEE-dee-uh**.

The construction is understandable after seeing it, but long in speech and vulnerable to predictable errors:

- **Curiousitypedia**, from the common misspelling of “curiosity”;
- **Curiouspedia**, from shortening the middle syllables;
- a two-word rendering of the name;
- confusion about whether the stress belongs on “curiosity” or “pedia.”

Use **CuriosityPedia** consistently in product and prose. Spoken-recall testing remains useful because the compound is long and can be shortened or misspelled.

### Confusion and distinctiveness risk

| Signal | Assessment | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Exact prior conceptual use | **High concern** | A 2018 Google Research paper uses “CuriosityPedia” for a community-curated web organized around questions and relationships between them. The product concept is materially adjacent, not merely an unrelated word mention. |
| Near-identical prior uses | **Moderate concern** | “Curiouspedia” has appeared as an encyclopedia-like curiosity blog and as a future-design knowledge/mapping platform. The spelling and learning context are close. |
| Wikipedia association | **Moderate concern** | “-pedia” quickly communicates reference knowledge, but can suggest an encyclopedia or a relationship with Wikipedia. The brand must not imply affiliation, community authorship, or comprehensive authority. |
| Descriptiveness | **Moderate concern** | The name explains the category well but may be a comparatively weak, crowded source identifier because both halves are suggestive or descriptive of the service. |
| Searchability | **Mixed** | The exact string currently has little general web competition, but the 2018 research result is highly relevant and could remain prominent. Misspellings lead toward existing “Curiouspedia” material. |
| International use | **Needs research** | The compound is English-led, eight syllables in the suggested pronunciation, and not equally natural across the product's supported languages. Native-speaker testing is still required. |

### Domain evidence

RDAP checks on July 18, 2026 returned HTTP 404 for:

- `curiositypedia.com`
- `curiositypedia.org`
- `curiositypedia.net`
- `curiositypedia.app`
- `curiositypedia.dev`
- `curiositypedia.io`

An RDAP 404 means no current registry object was returned; it is a promising registration signal, not a purchase guarantee. `curiositypedia.ai` could not be confirmed because its RDAP request timed out. Prices, premium status, registry holds, and registrar checkout availability were not tested. Do not publicize or depend on any domain until it has actually been registered.

Domain registration is explicitly deferred. The existing production URL remains in service.

### Basic trademark evidence

The official USPTO Trademark Search system was checked on July 18, 2026 using the wordmark terms:

- `curiositypedia` — no results;
- `curiouspedia` — no results;
- `curiosity pedia` — broad results for the separate component words, not an exact-match clearance result.

This is only a federal screening search. It does not cover all similar marks, state registrations, business names, foreign registers, unregistered common-law rights, or the legal effect of the documented prior uses. The USPTO itself advises that marks can conflict when they are similar in sound, appearance, meaning, or commercial impression and the goods or services are related. It recommends a comprehensive search across multiple sources.

## Product position and voice

These elements can survive a naming change and should guide all three directions below.

### Concise product description

**Turn one question into a source-backed path of discovery. Choose what to explore next, see how ideas branch, and return without losing the trail.**

### Short descriptor

**Branching, source-backed learning.**

This replaces “Curiosity performed,” which should not carry into the next identity.

### Positioning

For curious people who want to stay with a subject rather than collect isolated answers, the product is a guided research map that turns each answer into two meaningful next paths. Unlike a search-results list or an endless chat transcript, it preserves the evidence and the route the learner took.

### Voice principles

- **Inviting, not juvenile.** Make room for wonder without talking down to the reader.
- **Clear before clever.** State the answer or action plainly; let personality live in rhythm, examples, and details.
- **Evidence-aware, not institutionally certain.** Distinguish supported findings, interpretation, and uncertainty.
- **Specific, not breathless.** Prefer concrete objects, events, mechanisms, and questions over generic excitement.
- **Guiding, not controlling.** Offer compelling paths while preserving the user's agency.
- **Warmly editorial, not futuristic.** Feel like an excellent reference book, museum label, or field notebook—not an AI command console.

## Initial brand directions

These are approval-level concepts, not final logos. Image generation, mark refinement, accessibility testing, and export production begin only after the name decision.

### Direction A — The Open Index (recommended)

**Idea:** A living reference system in which every answer has open edges.

- **Mark territory:** A simple open book or index card whose negative space becomes a branching `Y`; alternatively, two page corners that form a question path. Avoid a literal question mark inside a book.
- **Wordmark:** Editorial serif with a calm sans-serif companion. If CuriosityPedia survives, give the long name generous spacing and do not split or camel-case it.
- **System:** Warm paper, deep green-black ink, acid highlight used sparingly for active paths, and coral/sky as branch identifiers.
- **Motion:** A line grows to a fork, then pauses for the learner's choice. Reduced-motion mode shows the completed fork without animation.
- **Strength:** Closest to the real product behavior and current editorial visual language.
- **Risk:** Book imagery can overstate encyclopedic completeness unless the open branch remains the dominant idea.

### Direction B — The Field Note

**Idea:** Curiosity as a trail of observations, evidence, and useful marginalia.

- **Mark territory:** A cropped annotation ring, evidence pin, or imperfect circular stamp intersected by a route line.
- **Wordmark:** Humanist sans-serif with a restrained serif for research titles.
- **System:** Cream stock, graphite green, muted sky, specimen-label rules, handwritten accents used only for nonessential decoration.
- **Motion:** Pins and notes resolve into a visible route; never simulate frantic scribbling.
- **Strength:** Human, credible, tactile, and compatible with factual images and citations.
- **Risk:** Can drift into scrapbook nostalgia or make the product feel manually curated rather than live and computational.

### Direction C — The Bright Branch

**Idea:** A playful decision tree that makes the next question feel irresistible.

- **Mark territory:** Two rounded paths emerging from one dot; modular nodes can become icons, avatars, or social-card frames.
- **Wordmark:** Friendly contemporary sans-serif; high legibility at small sizes takes priority over novelty.
- **System:** Dark ink ground with paper panels and stronger coral, sky, lavender, and acid branch colors.
- **Motion:** Nodes expand from the selected path with clear spatial continuity.
- **Strength:** Best expression of choice, replay, and a product that welcomes many ages.
- **Risk:** Without the editorial typography and evidence cues, it can resemble a children's quiz or generic mind-mapping app.

### Direction decision

Approve one visual territory independently of the final name. **Direction A, The Open Index, is recommended** because it combines the reference value implied by the candidate name with the genuinely differentiating branch behavior. Direction B is the strongest alternative if trust and evidence should dominate; Direction C is the strongest if broad approachability should dominate.

## Brand guardrails

- Do not use Wikipedia's puzzle-globe structure, a `W`, or visual language that suggests Wikimedia affiliation.
- Do not use a generic lightbulb, brain, rocket, magnifying glass, or question-mark mascot as the primary mark.
- Do not present generated art as evidence or let the identity blur the product's factual-image contract.
- Do not claim completeness, truth, neutrality, expert review, or safety without a defined and verifiable basis.
- Preserve multilingual legibility and do not make Latin-letter styling carry essential meaning.
- Use color as reinforcement, never the only indication of branch, state, confidence, or selection.
- Design the mark first for a 16–32 px app icon, a compact header, monochrome use, and social preview attribution.

## Rename migration checklist

This checklist is intentionally prepared now but must not be executed before owner approval.

### Product and code

- User-facing navigation, headings, empty states, loading states, errors, exports, emails, and authentication messages.
- Internal identifiers that are safe and worthwhile to rename separately from public copy; avoid unnecessary database or API breakage.
- Package metadata, application manifests, generated artifact configuration, test fixtures, snapshots, and accessibility labels.
- Locale source strings and every translation; review meaning rather than performing a blind token replacement.
- Prompt and model instructions where the product name affects generated output.

### Repository and documentation

- README, contributing and security material, architecture and code indexes, experience documentation, roadmap references, examples, and screenshots.
- File and directory names containing `curiositypedia`, including design sources and generation scripts; retain historical records where renaming would destroy provenance.
- Comments, test descriptions, environment examples, deployment instructions, and repository description/topics.
- A final case-insensitive search for `CuriosityPedia`, `curiositypedia`, spaced variants, the old `W` mark, and “Curiosity performed.” Classify intentional historical references rather than deleting them automatically.

### Web, deployment, and distribution

- Production and preview domains, DNS, certificates, redirects, canonical URLs, CORS/origin allowlists, OAuth callbacks, webhooks, CSP, and trusted-host settings.
- Hosting project/site names, environment variables, analytics properties, error tracking, status pages, support addresses, and monitoring labels.
- Page titles, descriptions, structured data, favicon/app icons, web manifest, Open Graph/Twitter images, share templates, and link previews.
- Search-console and webmaster properties, sitemap/robots references, redirects, and indexed old URLs.

### Brand and operations

- Wordmark, app icon, favicon, monochrome mark, lockups, spacing rules, color tokens, typography licenses, motion behavior, and accessible alternatives.
- Social handles, profile names, avatars, banners, marketplace listings, demo accounts, pitch material, and event assets.
- Domain ownership, trademark/counsel records, asset source files, export naming, and a single canonical brand package.
- A coordinated release plan so code, deployment, documentation, and public channels change together, with rollback instructions.
